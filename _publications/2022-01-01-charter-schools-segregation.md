abstract: 'We examine the effect of charter school expansion on racial segregation in public schools, using multiple measures of sorting and isolation. Exploiting between-grade variation in charter expansion as an instrumental variable, we find that charter schools modestly increase segregation overall. Charters have caused a 6 percent decrease in the relative likelihood of Black and Hispanic students being exposed to schoolmates of other racial or ethnic groups. However, within metropolitan areas, charters reduce segregation between school districts even as they increase it within districts.'
