abstract: 'I decompose school segregation in the United States into components attributable to residential segregation, school attendance boundaries, and school siting decisions. Using a counterfactual based on distance-minimizing school assignments, I find that attendance boundaries create 5 percent more integration than the distance-minimizing baseline, while school siting plays almost no role. Residential segregation alone explains more than 100 percent of school segregation in the United States. These results suggest that desegregation efforts focused solely on attendance boundaries and school siting are unlikely to substantially reduce racial sorting across schools.'
